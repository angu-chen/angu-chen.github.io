Foundations Reflection

A small blog to reflect on the human skills component of foundations

What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the Human Skills learning?

I've discovered I'm actually pretty good at connecting with teammates when I just focus on really listening to them instead of planning what I'll say next. It's been a game-changer for how I work with others. I also realized my ego isn't all bad—turns out those high standards I set for myself can actually push me to do better when I stop beating myself up and start seeing challenges as chances to grow. The biggest surprise was finding out that being honest about when I'm struggling doesn't make me look weak—it actually helps build trust. When I admitted I was completely lost during the tic-tac-toe challenge, I felt supported knowing I was not alone.

What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

Values provide a foundation for ethical decision-making in programming, helping us create software that serves people well rather than just meeting technical requirements. Empathy enables us to understand user needs deeply and collaborate effectively in teams. Self-awareness helps us recognize our strengths and weaknesses, manage stress during difficult debugging sessions, and maintain a growth mindset when learning complex concepts. Together, these human skills enhance our technical abilities by improving how we approach problems and work with others.

What has surprised you the most about the Human Skills learning?

The most surprising aspect has been discovering how directly applicable these "soft skills" are to technical work. I initially expected them to be supplementary, but I've found that emotional intelligence and communication skills are often what determine a project's success, not just technical knowledge. I was also surprised by how much my personal biases were influencing my approach to problem-solving without me realizing it.

What were the most challenging aspects of the Human Skills learning?

Actually thinking about it deeply

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on Human Skills learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

The programming school focuses on Human Skills because the tech industry has recognized that technical skills alone don't create successful developers. The majority of programming work happens in teams and involves understanding client needs, communicating complex ideas clearly, resolving conflicts, and collaborating on code. Additionally, as developers, we're building products for humans, so understanding human psychology and needs is essential for creating intuitive, accessible, and valuable software. These skills also prepare us for technical interviews and workplace interactions that often determine career advancement.

Does the time you spent studying Human Skills learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practise programming instead? Justify your answer.

No, the time spent on Human Skills doesn't feel wasted at all. While more programming practice would have improved my technical skills incrementally, the Human Skills learning has multiplied the effectiveness of my technical skills. Understanding how to communicate my ideas, collaborate with diverse teammates, manage my emotions during stressful debugging sessions, and approach problems with creativity has made me more effective at applying my technical knowledge. I've noticed that I learn programming concepts faster now because I'm more self-aware about my learning process and better at asking for help. In the long run, these skills will likely be the differentiator in my career advancement, as most developers eventually reach similar technical competence, but not all develop these crucial human capabilities.
